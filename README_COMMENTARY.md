# GitTone

My inspiration for this project came from wanting to hear software development as a process instead of reading it as documentation. Repositories are usually experienced through commit logs, graphs, diffs, and issue threads, but those views often hide the emotional rhythm of building: bursts of momentum, long stretches of revision, collaboration, friction, and release. I wanted to know whether the history of a project could become a piece of music that preserved those patterns. That led me to treat commits, pull requests, contributors, and CI runs as musical material rather than just as technical records.

The main challenge was balancing data fidelity with musical clarity. Repository activity is irregular. Some periods are overcrowded, some are almost silent, and a literal one-to-one translation quickly becomes noise. I had to decide what should be preserved, compressed, or stylized so that the result remained meaningful to a listener. That is why the project clips to the densest activity window, snaps events to a rhythmic grid, limits density in burst periods, and assigns each dataset a distinct musical role. A second challenge was harmonic coherence. Because six independent layers all produce sound simultaneously, it was easy for the mix to become dissonant. The solution was to make the pad layer the harmonic authority — its chord progression is scheduled first, and every other melodic layer consults the currently-sounding chord when it chooses a pitch. Commits, contributors, and PR bells all draw from the same chord tones at any given moment, so the music stays unified even though no single layer controls the whole.

A third challenge was representation without melodrama. Pull requests were initially long sustained drones whose durations tracked open-to-merge time, but in practice that produced an accumulating wash of sawtooth noise that overwhelmed the mix. The better solution was simpler: mark the merge moment as a bell chime whose richness reflects the size of the PR and whose brightness reflects the review outcome. A celebrated approval rings high and open; a hard-fought revision rings low and dark. That is still data-driven, but it fits the musical space rather than dominating it.

Over the course of this project, my perspective on the relationship between science and art has become integrated. I no longer see science as pure objectivity and art as pure feeling. Both rely on observation, pattern recognition, framing, and interpretation. Science helped me define systems, constraints, and repeatable mappings. Art helped me judge whether those systems communicated anything human. This project showed me that data can be both evidence and material, and that creative work can also be analytical. The same commit history that an engineer would read as a velocity chart can be heard as a melody if the mapping is designed with enough care for both sides. I now think science and art are different languages for investigating the same world, and that the most interesting problems live exactly at the border between them.
