# GitTone

My inspiration for this project came from wanting to hear software development as a process instead of reading it as documentation. Repositories are usually experienced through commit logs, graphs, diffs, and issue threads, but those views often hide the emotional rhythm of building: bursts of momentum, long stretches of revision, collaboration, friction, and release. I wanted to know whether the history of a project could become a piece of music that preserved those patterns. That led me to treat commits, pull requests, contributors, and CI runs as musical material rather than just as technical records.

The main challenge was balancing data fidelity with musical clarity. Repository activity is irregular. Some periods are overcrowded, some are almost silent, and a literal one-to-one translation quickly becomes noise. I had to decide what should be preserved, compressed, or stylized so that the result remained meaningful to a listener. That is why the project clips to the densest activity window, quantizes values into D dorian, limits event density, and assigns each dataset a different instrumental function. Another challenge was avoiding gimmickry. I did not want the piece to sound like statistics wearing a soundtrack. It needed enough structure, repetition, and atmosphere to feel composed while still remaining tied to the underlying history.

Over the course of this term, my perspective on science and art has become integrated. I no longer see science as pure objectivity and art as pure feeling. Both rely on observation, pattern recognition, framing, and interpretation. Science helped me define systems, constraints, and repeatable mappings. Art helped me judge whether those systems communicated anything human. This project showed me that data can be both evidence and material, and that creative work can also be analytical. I now think science and art are different languages for investigating the same world.
